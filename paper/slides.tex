\note{%
  \textbf{[0:00 — 0:30]}\\
  Hi everyone! I'm Joomy, a researcher at Bloomberg's CTO Infrastructure and
  Security Research team. Today I'm going to talk about extracting programs
  from proofs --- and specifically, about what happens where the target's
  type system runs out.\\[1ex]
  In dependently typed languages, we can prove things about our code; to
  \emph{run} that code, we extract it. And to be fair, extraction to OCaml
  does give us typed code. But wherever the dependent structure exceeds what
  OCaml's types can say, the extractor reaches for \texttt{Obj.magic}, an
  unchecked escape hatch --- and that escape hatch is exactly where the
  safety we proved leaks out. I'll show you a \emph{verified} translation
  from the Calculus of Constructions into a target that can carry more of
  the type structure --- System~F$\omega$ plus a dynamic type and blame ---
  where the remaining gap is an explicit, typed boundary instead of an
  unchecked cast. And all of it is mechanized in Rocq, with no admits.
}

%% ==========================================================================
\section{Motivation}
%% ==========================================================================

\begin{frame}{Extraction runs out of types}
  Dependently typed languages (Rocq, Agda, Lean, Idris) let us \emph{prove}
  properties of code. To \emph{run} it, extraction erases the static parts:
  \[
    \text{Gallina} \;\longrightarrow\; \text{OCaml / Haskell / Scheme}
  \]

  \pause
  \medskip
  This makes \alert{two concessions}:
  \begin{enumerate}
    \item<3-> Dependent invariants \alert<3>{exceed the target's types} ---
      the gap is bridged by \alert<3>{\texttt{Obj.magic}}.
    \item<4-> The extractor is \alert<4>{trusted}, not verified.
  \end{enumerate}

  \onslide<5->{%
  \medskip
  Forster--Sozeau--Tabareau (PLDI~'24) fix \#2. \quad\textbf{We tackle \#1.}}
  \note{%
    \textbf{[0:30 — 2:00]}\\
    Let's start with the setting. In Rocq, Agda, Lean, or Idris, we prove
    properties of our code. But to run that code, we extract it to OCaml,
    Haskell, or Scheme --- and extraction erases all the static parts. Now,
    this \click costs us two things.\\
    \click First, the target's types can't say what the source's types said.
    Your ``vector of length \texttt{n}'' becomes just a list --- the proof
    that made it safe is gone from the type. And actually, it's worse than
    that: where ML polymorphism can't bridge the gap, the extractor inserts
    \texttt{Obj.magic}, an \emph{unchecked} cast. I'll show you what that
    costs on the next slide.\\
    \click And second, the extractor itself joins the trusted computing
    base --- its correctness is usually argued on paper, not proved.\\
    \click There is great recent work by Forster, Sozeau, and Tabareau on the
    \emph{second} problem: a verified extractor --- but to an untyped target.
    This talk is about the \emph{first} problem: can we extract into a
    \emph{typed} target and keep the structure?
  }
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}[fragile]{Not just untyped --- \alert{unsafe}}
  Type-safe \texttt{printf}: the format \emph{value} determines the type.\\
  \src{\texttt{sprintf : Fmt A -> A}}, \quad
  \src{\texttt{fint : Fmt A -> Fmt (Nat -> A)}}.\\[0.4ex]
  Wrong arity $=$ type error.

  \bigskip
  \pause
  Rocq 9.0 extraction severs the link:
\begin{lstlisting}[language={},basicstyle=\ttfamily\footnotesize]
type 'x fmt = Fstop | Flit of str * 'x fmt
            | Fint of __ fmt | Fstr of __ fmt   (* 'x: unconstrained *)
val sprintf : 'a1 fmt -> 'a1                    (* 'a1: anything     *)
\end{lstlisting}
  \note{%
    \textbf{[2:00 — 2:45]}\\
    Let me show you the concrete version of that first concession --- and
    this is an accident anyone can have, not an attack. Here is type-safe
    printf --- source code, which I'll always show in blue. The format
    \emph{value} determines the type of the function it denotes: a format
    with one percent-d gives you a one-argument function, and using a format
    at the wrong arity is a \emph{type error}. That is the whole point of
    this example. Now, \click let's extract it. The format type's parameter
    is no longer constrained by its constructors --- look at \texttt{Fint}:
    it takes any format and returns any format. So \texttt{sprintf} comes out
    at ``for any \texttt{a}, \texttt{fmt} to \texttt{a}'': the format no
    longer determines the type --- the \emph{caller} does. The invariant is
    gone, and an \texttt{Obj.magic} inside \texttt{sprintf} vouches for
    whatever the caller assumed.
  }
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}[fragile]{An honest refactoring, a segfault}
\begin{lstlisting}[language={},basicstyle=\ttfamily\footnotesize]
(* the format says "%d"; this call site believes "%d%s" *)
let report : nat -> str -> str = sprintf (Fint Fstop)
let _ = report O Sempty
\end{lstlisting}

  \pause
  \bigskip
  \alert{Segmentation fault.} Not catchable. Names nobody.

  \bigskip
  \onslide<3->{7 of our 18 example idioms extract with \texttt{Obj.magic} /
  \texttt{Obj.t}.\\[0.8ex]
  This talk: failure $=$ $\blame{p}$ --- \emph{typed}, observable,
  attributable.\\[0.4ex]
  ``The worst case is blame'' is a \textcolor{okGreen}{theorem}.}
  \note{%
    \textbf{[2:45 — 3:30]}\\
    So now this happens. The format says percent-d; the call site believes it
    says percent-d percent-s --- someone added an argument in one place and
    not the other. An honest, everyday refactoring drift --- the exact bug
    type-safe printf exists to rule out. And OCaml accepts it, because
    \texttt{sprintf} returns whatever type you like. What happens when we run
    it? \click A segmentation fault. Not an exception --- a segfault. You
    cannot catch it, and nothing tells you which boundary was violated.\\
    \click And this is not rare: seven of our eighteen example idioms hit
    this. So here is the pitch of this talk: let's make that boundary a
    \emph{typed} cast, so that a failure is \emph{blame} --- an ordinary,
    observable outcome with a label that names the boundary --- and let's
    make ``the worst case is blame'' a machine-checked theorem of the target
    language. And by the way: whenever you see green in these slides, like
    this ``theorem'' here, it is a real machine-checked theorem in Rocq, not
    just a claim.
  }
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{The question, and this work}
  \begin{center}
    \large
    How much of a dependently typed program's structure can we
    \alert{preserve} when we extract into a richer-typed target ---\\[0.5ex]
    can the fallback where its types run out be \alert{safe} ---\\[0.5ex]
    and can the translation be \alert{proved correct}?
  \end{center}

  \pause
  \medskip
  \begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Source:} \src{the Calculus of Constructions} --- a pure
      type system (Barras' \emph{Coq-in-Coq}).
    \item<3-> \textbf{Target:} \tgt{System~F$\omega$} $+$ $\dyn$ $+$ casts $+$
      \textcolor{blameRed}{blame}, at higher kinds.
    \item<4-> \textbf{Lineage:} Paulin-Mohring (POPL~'89), now with an
      explicit boundary.
  \end{itemize}
  \note{%
    \textbf{[3:30 — 4:30]}\\
    So the question, stated plainly, has three parts: how much structure
    survives if the target's types are richer? Where its types still run
    out, can the fallback be \emph{safe} --- an explicit, checked failure
    instead of \texttt{Obj.magic}? And can we \emph{prove} the whole
    translation right? Here's the setup. \click Our source is the Calculus of Constructions ---
    full dependent types, presented as a pure type system, and mechanized on
    top of Barras' classic Coq-in-Coq development.\\
    \click Our target is System~F$\omega$ --- so, higher-kinded
    polymorphism --- plus a dynamic type Dyn, casts, and blame. That is a
    \emph{polymorphic blame calculus}, in the Ahmed--Findler--Siek--Wadler
    tradition, but lifted to higher kinds.\\
    \click And there's some lineage here: Paulin-Mohring extracted F$\omega$
    programs from CoC proofs back in '89. We are returning to that design
    point, but with an explicit dynamic-slash-blame boundary for the parts
    F$\omega$ can't reach.
  }
\end{frame}

\section{The idea, by example}
%% ==========================================================================

\begin{frame}{The idea: be \emph{optimistic}}
  \tgt{F$\omega$} already preserves a lot: type abstraction, type application,
  higher-kinded families. \quad \src{CoC} has strictly more: \alert{full
  dependency} --- types may mention terms.

  \bigskip
  At the boundary, exactly three things happen:
  \begin{itemize}
    \item<2-> \tgt{\textbf{Preserve}} type structure.
    \item<3-> \textbf{Erase} term indices from types:
      \src{\texttt{Vec A n}} $\rightsquigarrow$ \tgt{\texttt{Vec A}}.
    \item<4-> \textcolor{dynOrange}{\textbf{Dynamize}} values used \emph{as
      types}: \src{\texttt{El : U -> Set}} $\rightsquigarrow$ $\dyn$ $+$
      \textcolor{blameRed}{blame}.
  \end{itemize}

  \onslide<5->{%
  \bigskip
  \begin{block}{Optimistic}
    Never defensively dynamize what F$\omega$ \emph{can} express. Reach for
    $\dyn$ only for the genuine residue of dependency.
  \end{block}}
  \note{%
    \textbf{[4:30 — 6:00]}\\
    This slide is the heart of the talk. F$\omega$ already gives us a lot:
    polymorphism, type-level functions, higher-kinded families. But CoC gives
    us \emph{more} --- types can mention \emph{terms}. So the translation has
    to decide what to do with that extra dependency. And exactly three things
    can happen; I'll show you an example of each in a moment.\\[1ex]
    First, \click we preserve: anything that's genuinely type structure stays
    type structure. Second, \click we erase: an index like the \emph{length}
    of a vector is dropped \emph{from the type} --- \texttt{Vec A n} becomes
    \texttt{Vec A}. The value \texttt{n} may still be around as data; it's
    the \emph{invariant} that leaves. And third, \click we dynamize: the hard
    residue --- a \emph{value used as a type}, like a decoder
    \texttt{El : U -> Set} --- goes through Dyn, with blame. Two colors to
    keep an eye on from here: Dyn, the dynamic type, is always in orange ---
    that's our boundary --- and blame, a run-time cast failure, is always in
    red.\\
    \click The word ``optimistic'' is our design principle: we never dynamize
    defensively. We reach for Dyn \emph{only} where F$\omega$ genuinely
    cannot express the thing.
  }
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}[fragile]{Preserve: the type that catches the drift}
  The \texttt{sprintf} \alert{that segfaulted} --- now through \emph{our}
  extraction:
\begin{lstlisting}
Check sprintf (Nat -> Str) (fint Str fstop).
\end{lstlisting}
  \pause
\begin{lstlisting}[style=replout]
Inferred type: Nat -> Str
\end{lstlisting}

  \pause
  \medskip
  The computed arity is an ordinary \tgt{F$\omega$ arrow type} --- the
  drifted call site is a \alert{type error} again.
  \note{%
    \textbf{[6:00 — 6:45]}\\
    Let's start with \emph{preserve} --- and it's an old friend: the printf
    that segfaulted at the start of the talk, now going through our
    extraction. We ask for the type of a format with one percent-d.
    \click And the inferred target type is an ordinary arrow: Nat to Str.
    One argument. The format's computed arity survives \emph{in the type},
    because the target is polymorphic enough to keep it --- by the way, the
    teal box is extracted target output; blue source, teal target, for the
    rest of the talk. \click So the drifted call site from the segfault
    slide --- the one that passed two arguments --- is a \emph{type error}
    again, exactly as it was in the source. What OCaml's types could not
    say, the target's types can.
  }
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}[fragile,shrink]{Erase: term indices leave the types}
  A bounds-checked vector lookup, \src{\texttt{vnth}} --- an ordinary
  \texttt{Definition}:
\begin{lstlisting}
Definition vnth (A : Set) (n : Nat) (xs : Vec A n) (i : Fin n)
  : A :=
  vrec A (fun (m : Nat) => Fin m -> A)
    (fun (i0 : Fin NZ) => fin0 A i0)
    (fun (m : Nat) (a : A) (rest : Fin m -> A) (j : Fin (NS m)) =>
       fin_case m A a rest j)
    n xs i.
\end{lstlisting}
  \pause
\begin{lstlisting}[style=replout]
fun (A : *) (n : Nat) (xs : Vec A) (i : Fin) =>
  vrec [A] [Fin -> A]
    (fun (x0 : Fin) => fin0 [A] x0)
    (fun (x0 : Nat) (x1 : A) (x2 : Fin -> A) (x3 : Fin) =>
       fin_case x0 [A] x1 x2 x3)
    n xs i
\end{lstlisting}
  \pause
  \src{\texttt{Vec A n}} $\rightsquigarrow$ \tgt{\texttt{Vec A}}, \
  \src{\texttt{Fin n}} $\rightsquigarrow$ \tgt{\texttt{Fin}}: the
  \alert{invariant} is gone, the code survives --- and \alert{no unchecked
  cast} to hide behind.
  \note{%
    \textbf{[6:45 — 7:30]}\\
    Next, \emph{erase}. \texttt{vnth} looks up the \texttt{i}-th element of a
    length-\texttt{n} vector, where \texttt{i} is provably in range --- it's
    a \texttt{Fin n}. Just an ordinary definition built from the recursor.
    And here is \click its extraction. Look at the types in the teal box:
    \click \texttt{Vec A n} became \texttt{Vec A}, and \texttt{Fin n} became
    \texttt{Fin}. The dependent index dropped out of the \emph{types}; what
    we lost is the invariant that says \texttt{i} is in bounds. But look at
    what is \emph{not} here: there is no unchecked cast anywhere. Stock
    extraction hides an erased invariant like this behind an
    \texttt{Obj.magic}; in this target, there is nothing to hide behind ---
    every cast is checked, so no well-typed client of this code can cause
    undefined behavior. The worst thing that can happen anywhere in this
    language is an explicit blame --- and we'll make that a theorem in a few
    slides. Also notice the \texttt{[A]} in brackets --- those are type
    applications, so the polymorphism is fully preserved.
  }
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}[fragile]{Dynamize: where the dynamic type earns its keep}
  A Tarski universe: codes \src{\texttt{U}}, decoder \src{\texttt{El : U -> Set}}
  --- a function from \emph{data} to \emph{types}.
\begin{lstlisting}
Extract fun (c : U) => El c.
\end{lstlisting}
  \pause
\begin{lstlisting}[style=replout]
fun (c : U) =>
  cast<Dyn => U -> Dyn>^2+ (blame(2+)) c
\end{lstlisting}
  \pause
  \begin{itemize}
    \item \src{\texttt{El}} maps values to types --- \alert{no F$\omega$
      image}.
    \item \textcolor{blameRed}{Internal blame} (label $2$) through $\dyn$:
      an \emph{honest} ``I can't translate this.''
    \item<4-> Theorem: isolated runs \alert{never} blame an \emph{external}
      label.
  \end{itemize}
  \note{%
    \textbf{[7:30 — 9:00]}\\
    Now let's look at dynamize --- the interesting case. Here we have a
    Tarski universe: \texttt{U} is a type of \emph{codes}, and \texttt{El}
    decodes a code into an actual type. So \texttt{El} sends \emph{data} to
    \emph{types} --- and F$\omega$ has nothing like that. So what does the
    extraction of \texttt{fun c => El c} look like? \click It looks like
    this. \click Since \texttt{El} has no F$\omega$ counterpart, we don't
    fudge it --- we plant a \emph{blame}, cast through Dyn. It's a marked,
    honest failure that says: this program used a value as a type, and I
    cannot express that. The label \texttt{2+} tells you \emph{which} kind of
    failure this is --- I'll unpack the labels in two slides.\\
    \click And crucially, we \emph{prove} that a term we extract, run on its
    own, only ever trips these \emph{internal} markers --- never an external,
    user-level blame. So the fallback is contained.
  }
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}[fragile,shrink]{The example you'd expect to need
    \texorpdfstring{$\dyn$}{Dyn} --- and doesn't}
  CIC's poster child for a \emph{computed} return type:
\begin{lstlisting}
Fixpoint eval {t} (e : expr t)
  : match t with TNat => nat | TBool => bool end := ...
\end{lstlisting}
  \pause
  Church-encode the codes \alert{one kind up} ($\kstar\karr\kstar\karr\kstar$):
  \texttt{match} becomes type-level \emph{application} $t\,\mathrm{CNat}\,\mathrm{CBool}$.
\begin{lstlisting}[style=replout]
fun (t : (* => (* => *)))
    (x : t (forall (x0 : *), (x0 -> x0) -> x0 -> x0)
           (forall (x0 : *), x0 -> x0 -> x0)) =>
  x
\end{lstlisting}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item<3-> Extracts with \alert{no $\dyn$, no casts, no blame} --- yet it
      is stock extraction's heaviest \texttt{Obj.magic} user.
    \item<4-> Forcing $\dyn$ as a return type needs \alert{large elimination}
      --- the CIC extension.
  \end{itemize}
  \note{%
    \textbf{[9:00 — 10:30]}\\
    Now, a bit of a gotcha slide. The program everyone expects to
    \emph{force} a dynamic type is the tagless interpreter: its \emph{return
    type} is computed by a \texttt{match} on a run-time tag --- \texttt{nat}
    or \texttt{bool}.\\
    \click But if you Church-encode the type codes one kind \emph{up}, that
    \texttt{match} becomes ordinary type-level \emph{application}, and
    F$\omega$ eats it for free.\\
    \click The whole interpreter extracts with \emph{no} Dyn, \emph{no}
    casts, \emph{no} blame --- it lands squarely in the static fragment. And
    I love the contrast here: this very program is the heaviest
    \texttt{Obj.magic} user in our whole example suite under stock OCaml
    extraction --- and here it needs nothing at all, because the target has
    the kinds to say what OCaml's types cannot.\\
    \click You only truly force Dyn as a computed \emph{return} type when the
    source has \emph{large elimination} --- which bare CoC lacks. So the
    boundary is sharper than the folklore suggests. And if I'm short on time,
    I'll just say that sentence and move on.
  }
\end{frame}

\section{The target: F\texorpdfstring{$\omega$}{omega} + blame}
%% ==========================================================================

\begin{frame}{Target syntax}
  \begin{align*}
    K &::= \kstar \mid K \karr K &&\text{(kinds)}\\
    \onslide<2->
    A, B &::= \alpha \mid A \to B \mid \forall \alpha{:}K.\,A
      \mid \Lambda \alpha{:}K.\,A \mid A\ B \mid \dyn &&\text{(types)}\\
    \onslide<3->
    e &::= x \mid \lambda x{:}A.\,e \mid e\ e
      \mid \Lambda \alpha{:}K.\,e \mid e\,[A]\\
    &\quad \mid \castt{e}{A}{B}{p} \mid \gnd{e}{G} \mid \isgnd{e}{G}
      \mid \blame{p} \mid \nub{\alpha}{K}{A}{e} &&\text{(terms)}
  \end{align*}

  \begin{itemize}
    \item<4-> $\dyn$ lives at kind $\kstar$ only.
    \item<5-> Ground tags now include \alert{neutral} types
      $N ::= \alpha \mid N\,A$ --- the F$\omega$ lift.
  \end{itemize}
  \note{%
    \textbf{[10:30 — 12:00]}\\
    Here is the whole target in one slide, revealed line by line. The kinds
    are the usual F$\omega$ kinds. \click The types are F$\omega$ types ---
    variables, arrows, foralls, type-level lambdas, applications --- plus
    \emph{Dyn}. \click And the terms are F$\omega$ terms plus four
    gradual-typing gadgets: a \emph{cast} from A to B carrying a label; a
    \emph{ground injection} and a \emph{ground test} that mediate Dyn;
    \emph{blame} itself; and a $\nu$ binder that seals a type --- I'll come
    back to that one.\\[1ex]
    Two design points are worth flagging here. \click First, Dyn lives only
    at kind star --- there is no \(\mathrm{Dyn}_K\) for every kind.\\
    \click And second, the ``ground types'' --- the shapes Dyn can hold ---
    now include \emph{neutral} types: a type variable applied to arguments.
    That's the lift from System~F to F$\omega$: once type \emph{applications}
    can sit at the Dyn boundary, the tags have to cover them. Keep that in
    mind; it motivates the next two slides.
  }
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}[shrink]{Reading a blame label}
  A failing cast never gets stuck, never lies --- it fires $\blame{p}$:
  \begin{center}
  \begin{tabular}{ll}
    \textbf{id} & \emph{which} cast boundary broke \\
    \textbf{polarity} & \emph{whose} fault: \ $+$ the value in the cast, \
      $-$ the context around it \\
  \end{tabular}
  \end{center}

  \onslide<2->{%
  \smallskip
  Three ids are \alert{reserved} for the translation's own plumbing:
  \begin{center}
  \begin{tabular}{cl}
    \toprule
    id & fires when \ldots \\
    \midrule
    \textcolor{blameRed}{0} & a sealed type ($\nu$) would escape its scope through $\dyn$ \\
    \textcolor{blameRed}{1} & the ground test $\mathsf{is\_gnd}$ peeks at a sealed value \\
    \textcolor{blameRed}{2} & the extraction hits a construct with \alert{no F$\omega$ image} \ (${=}\,\lint$) \\
    \tgt{$\ge 3$} & \tgt{external} --- a genuine boundary a client set up \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
  \end{center}}

  \onslide<3->{%
  \begin{alertblock}{The safety theorem, in one line}
    An extracted term run in isolation only blames \textcolor{blameRed}{0},
    \textcolor{blameRed}{1}, or \textcolor{blameRed}{2} --- its own plumbing ---
    \alert{never} an external label ($\ge 3$).
  \end{alertblock}}
  \note{%
    \textbf{[12:00 — 14:00]}\\
    Let's demystify blame labels; people often ask about these, so I'll take
    my time here. When a cast cannot make its two types agree, it does not
    get stuck, and it does not lie --- it fires \texttt{blame p}. The label
    is a name tag on the failure, and it answers two questions. The
    \emph{id} says which cast boundary broke. The \emph{polarity} --- a plus
    or a minus --- says whose fault it was: plus blames the value flowing
    through the cast, minus blames the surrounding context. That's the
    Wadler--Findler convention; a function cast flips the sign on its
    argument.\\[1ex]
    \click Now, the numbers. Most ids name real, programmer-level boundaries.
    But three small ids are reserved for our own machinery. Zero fires when a
    sealed type variable tries to escape its scope through Dyn. One fires
    when the ground test peeks at a sealed value. And two is the big one from
    the \texttt{El} slide: the extraction hit something with no F$\omega$
    image. Anything three or above is \emph{external} --- a genuine boundary
    that a client set up.\\
    \click And that split is the whole point: we prove that an extracted
    term, run in isolation, can only ever blame zero, one, or two --- its own
    plumbing --- and never an external label. In other words, the extraction
    never frames an outside party. And compare this with the segfault at the
    start of the talk, which named nobody: here, when a client sets up a
    boundary and violates it, the failing cast carries the client's label,
    so the failure names its culprit.
  }
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}[shrink]{Executable compatibility: no stuck casts}
  Unlike Blame-for-All's \emph{consistency}, our $\mathsf{compat}(A,B)$ is
  \alert{executable} --- every constructor \emph{is} a cast-reduction rule:
  \begin{center}\small
  \begin{tabular}{ll}
    \toprule
    constructor & reduction rule \\
    \midrule
    \tgt{$\mathsf{compat\_refl}$} & \textcolor{accentOrange}{ID} \\
    \tgt{$\mathsf{compat\_arrow}$} & \textcolor{accentOrange}{WRAP} (contra/covariant) \\
    \tgt{$\mathsf{compat\_all}$} / \tgt{generalize} / \tgt{instantiate} & \textcolor{accentOrange}{$\forall$-steps} (instantiate at $\kstar$) \\
    \tgt{$\mathsf{compat\_to\_dyn}$} / \tgt{from\_dyn} & \textcolor{accentOrange}{GROUND / COLLAPSE / CONFLICT} \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
  \end{center}

  \begin{itemize}
    \item<2-> To $\dyn$: inject at a ground tag. From $\dyn$: collapse or
      \textcolor{blameRed}{blame}.
    \item<3-> \alert{No stuck casts}: \mech{progress} via \mech{compat\_dec};
      $\mathsf{coerce}$ is \emph{total}.
    \item<4-> Caveat: syntactic --- not closed under definitional equality.
  \end{itemize}
  \note{%
    \textbf{[14:00 — 15:30]}\\
    Now, how do casts actually run? In System~F, Blame-for-All's
    ``consistency'' relation can be read both as a spec and as a strategy.
    Once you add type-level functions, that stops being obvious. So we make
    it \emph{executable}: every constructor of our \texttt{compat} relation
    corresponds to a concrete reduction rule --- identity casts, the function
    wrap rule, the $\forall$ steps, and the Dyn injection and projection
    rules.\\
    \click The Dyn mechanics go like this: casting \emph{to} Dyn injects the
    value at a ground tag; casting \emph{from} Dyn checks the tag --- it
    collapses if the tag matches, and it \emph{blames} if it doesn't. That
    blame-on-mismatch is where all the runtime failures come from.\\
    \click And the payoff: there is no catch-all ``stuck cast'' case. Every
    well-typed cast has a step, so progress essentially falls out of the
    decision procedure for \texttt{compat} --- and the same decidability
    makes the extraction's coercion function \emph{total}.\\
    \click One honest caveat: \texttt{compat} is syntactic --- it doesn't see
    through definitional equality. Keep that in mind; it matters in a minute.
  }
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Target metatheory --- all mechanized, admit-free}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item<1-> \textbf{Determinism} (\mech{step\_deterministic}) --- proving
      it caught a real rule overlap.
    \item<2-> \textbf{Type-level confluence \& Church--Rosser}.
    \item<3-> \textbf{Progress \& preservation} (\mech{progress},
      \mech{preservation}) --- ``the \alert{worst case is blame},'' as a
      theorem.
    \item<4-> \alert{Not} strongly normalizing
      (\mech{well\_typed\_non\_normalizing}): safety $\ne$ termination.
  \end{itemize}
  \note{%
    \textbf{[15:30 — 16:30]}\\
    The target is a real, self-standing calculus, and we mechanized its
    metatheory, all admit-free. First, determinism --- and proving it
    actually caught a genuine bug: two rules overlapped, and we fixed it with
    a side condition. \click Then type-level confluence and Church--Rosser,
    because we have honest F$\omega$ type computation. \click Then progress
    and preservation, for the \emph{whole} reduction relation, with kind
    regularity of every annotation. And notice: this pair is the promise
    from the segfault slide, discharged. A well-typed program in this target
    never has undefined behavior --- it's a value, it steps, or it blames.
    The worst case is blame, and now that is a theorem.\\
    \click And one deliberate non-result: the target is \emph{not} strongly
    normalizing --- there is a closed, well-typed loop. And that is fine!
    Type \emph{safety} does not require termination. The source, CoC,
    \emph{is} normalizing, and we use that separately.
  }
\end{frame}

\section{The extraction \& its metatheory}

\begin{frame}{The extraction: three maps, one invariant}
  \begin{description}
    \item<1->[Kinds] \src{$\SetS \to \Nat \to \SetS$} $\;\rightsquigarrow\;$
      \tgt{$\kstar \karr \kstar$} --- term domains drop.
    \item<2->[Types] \alert{normalize first}, then translate; sorts / term
      variables / term-headed applications $\rightsquigarrow \dyn$.
    \item<3->[Terms] $\ext$ recurses on the \alert{typing derivation}.
  \end{description}

  \onslide<4->{%
  \medskip
  \begin{block}{The invariant that makes it well defined}
    Every type-vs-term choice uses the reduction-stable, \emph{semantic} test
    $\islarge$ --- the translation \alert{respects definitional equality}.
  \end{block}}
  \note{%
    \textbf{[16:30 — 18:00]}\\
    The translation itself is three maps working together. On kinds: a source
    binder whose domain is type-level becomes a kind arrow, and a term-level
    domain is dropped --- that's how \texttt{Set -> Nat -> Set} becomes just
    star-to-star, the kind of a vector family.\\
    \click On types: the key move is to \emph{normalize first}, then
    translate. Sorts, term variables, and term-headed applications are what
    turn into Dyn.\\
    \click On terms: the extraction recurses on the \emph{typing derivation},
    not on the raw term --- it needs the typing information to classify the
    binders. If anyone asks: this is what forced \texttt{has\_type} to live
    in \texttt{Type} rather than \texttt{Prop}, and it dragged the whole
    reduction and conversion layer into \texttt{Type} with it, because Rocq
    forbids eliminating \texttt{Prop} into \texttt{Type}.\\[1ex]
    \click And here is the invariant that makes the whole thing well defined:
    every time we ask ``is this type-level or term-level?'', we use a
    \emph{semantic}, reduction-stable test --- does it have sort Kind? ---
    and that test is preserved by reduction. That is why the translation
    respects definitional equality instead of being brittle syntax.
  }
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{The hard case: it commutes only \emph{up to} conversion}
  For a large application (a type application, $\Lambda$-elimination), extraction
  emits a \alert{bare} $e\,[A]$ --- \emph{no} runtime cast. Why is that sound?

  \medskip
  Its natural type and the expected type,
  \[
    \extt(V{:}\Gamma, U_r)[\extt(\Gamma,v)/0]
    \qquad\text{vs.}\qquad
    \extt(\Gamma,\, U_r[v]),
  \]
  \pause
  are \alert{not syntactically equal}: a $\beta$-redex $+$ $\nf$ drift. With
  $=$, the lemma is \emph{false}.

  \pause
  \medskip
  They \emph{are} equal up to \mech{ty\_equiv} --- the conversion rule
  \texttt{typing\_conv} absorbs the gap. That is \emph{why} the target has
  one.
  \note{%
    \textbf{[18:00 — 19:30]}\\
    This one is my favorite subtlety, so let me take it slowly. When the
    extraction hits a \emph{type} application, it just emits a bare type
    application in the target --- no cast, no coercion. And you'd want to
    justify that with a lemma that says ``extraction commutes with
    substitution.'' Here are the two sides of that lemma.\\
    \click But phrased with plain \emph{equality}, the lemma is
    \emph{false}! The two sides differ in two ways: plugging in a large
    argument creates a target $\beta$-redex on one side, and normalizing
    annotations makes them drift on the other.\\
    \click The fix: the two sides \emph{are} equal up to F$\omega$
    definitional equality. So the extraction leans on the target's
    \emph{conversion} rule to absorb the mismatch --- and that is the real
    reason the target has to carry a conversion rule at all. This is exactly
    the kind of thing you only find out when you actually mechanize the
    proof.
  }
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{The headline theorems (Rocq; only \texttt{eq\_rect\_eq})}
  \begin{enumerate}
    \item<1-> \textbf{Well-typed} (\mech{extract\_well\_typed}):
      $\hastype{\extc(\Gamma)}{\ext(\Gamma \vdash t{:}T)}{\extt(\Gamma, T)}$.
    \item<2-> \textbf{Simulation} (\mech{extract\_reduces}): $t \reduces v$
      $\Rightarrow$ $\ext(t) \sepsim^{*} \ext(v)$.
    \item<3-> \textbf{Derivation independence} (\mech{extract\_deriv\_indep}).
    \item<4-> \textbf{No external blame in isolation}
      (\mech{extraction\_blame\_free}): never $\mathrm{id}(p) \ge 3$.
    \item<5-> \textbf{$\dyn$-freedom, exactly}
      (\mech{extract\_typ\_dyn\_free\_iff}).
  \end{enumerate}
  \note{%
    \textbf{[19:30 — 21:00]}\\
    And now the payoff theorems --- all in Rocq, and the audited set rests on
    a single standard axiom, \texttt{eq\_rect\_eq}. They come one per
    click.\\
    One: extraction is \emph{well-typed} --- a well-typed source term
    extracts to a well-typed target term, at the extracted type.\\
    \click Two: \emph{simulation} --- source reduction is tracked by the
    extraction.\\
    \click Three: derivation independence --- the result doesn't depend on
    which typing derivation you picked.\\
    \click Four: the safety result from the labels slide --- no external
    blame in isolation.\\
    \click And five: optimism, made precise --- an \emph{exact}
    characterization of which source types come out Dyn-free. I won't grind
    through all five out loud; the ones to remember are well-typedness,
    simulation, and label safety.
  }
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Being honest about what this is not}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item<1-> \textbf{Simulation is \emph{syntactic}} --- not contextual
      approximation. The typed logical-relations bridge is open.
    \item<2-> \textbf{Internal blame is reachable by design} --- but defined
      and observable, unlike \texttt{Obj.magic}.
    \item<3-> \textbf{No proof erasure; $\dyn$ at $\kstar$ only; inductives
      axiomatized.}
  \end{itemize}

  \onslide<4->{%
  \medskip
  None of these weakens the theorems proved --- they scope the claims.}
  \note{%
    \textbf{[21:00 — 22:00]}\\
    Let me be upfront about the boundaries of this work. The most important
    caveat: our \emph{simulation} relation is syntactic bookkeeping, not a
    behavioral, contextual approximation --- it deliberately contains rules
    that would be false as observational principles. Building the typed
    logical-relations bridge is open work, and I'd rather say that plainly.\\
    \click Second, internal blame is \emph{reachable} --- if you actually run
    a value-as-type, you get an internal blame. That is the honest fallback,
    by design --- and unlike an \texttt{Obj.magic} failure, it is a defined,
    observable outcome.\\
    \click And third, this is not proof-erasing, Dyn lives only at kind star,
    and inductives are axiomatized, since the source is a bare PTS. Those are
    all natural next steps.\\
    \click None of this weakens the theorems we proved --- it scopes the
    claims.
  }
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{\texttt{Inductive} by Böhm--Berarducci}
  \texttt{Inductive} desugars to the \alert{indexed} impredicative encoding:
  \[
    \tgt{\texttt{Vec A n}} \;=\; \forall (X{:}\Nat{\to}\kstar).\;
      X\,\mathrm{NZ} \to (\forall m.\,A \to X\,m \to X\,(\mathrm{NS}\,m)) \to X\,n
  \]

  \onslide<2->{%
  \medskip
  The motive is \alert{index-parameterized} ($X : I{\to}\kstar$), so
  \tgt{Vec}, \tgt{Fin}, \tgt{Eq} ($\mathit{Eq\_rec}$ \emph{is} transport),
  \tgt{Fmt}, $\le$ all \textbf{compute}.}
  \note{%
    \textbf{[23:00 — 23:45]}\\
    Here's a bonus that the REPL gives us: an \texttt{Inductive} command that
    is pure sugar for the impredicative Böhm--Berarducci encoding --- and it
    handles \emph{indexed} families. \click Because the motive is
    parameterized by the index, Vec, Fin, Leibniz equality --- whose recursor
    is literally transport --- the format family, the less-or-equal
    predicate: they all become plain lambda terms that genuinely
    \emph{compute}. Böhm--Berarducci did this in '85; Pfenning and Paulin
    encoded these in CoC in '90.
  }
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{\ldots and its ceiling}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item \alert{Ceiling 1}: the recursor is a fold, not dependent
      \emph{induction} (Geuvers '01) --- no large elimination.
    \item<2-> \alert{Ceiling 2}: one-step \emph{inversion} is not a fold ---
      kept axiomatic.
    \item<3-> Scott/Parigot need recursive types; Mendler: same ceiling;
      \textcolor{okGreen}{self types} (Fu--Stump '14) go beyond CoC.
  \end{itemize}
  \note{%
    \textbf{[23:45 — 24:30]}\\
    Now, the honest ceiling --- and it's intrinsic, not a bug. The generated
    recursor's motive depends on the \emph{indices} but not on the
    \emph{value}. It's a fold, not dependent induction --- and Geuvers proved
    in 2001 that induction simply is not derivable from these encodings. So
    large elimination and dependent projections are out of reach.\\
    \click Second, one-step inversion is not a fold either, so those stay
    axiomatic.\\
    \click And you might say, but Joomy, what about the other encodings?
    They don't save us: Scott and Parigot give you constant-time inversion,
    but their types are recursive, so they don't even typecheck in a
    normalizing system without mu-types; Mendler handles more functors but
    hits the same wall; and if you really want dependent elimination that
    computes, that's self types --- Fu and Stump --- which is a genuine
    extension beyond CoC. So this is the typeability-versus-computability
    tension in one picture.
  }
\end{frame}
