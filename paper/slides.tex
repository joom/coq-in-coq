  \textbf{[0:00 — 0:30]}\\
  Hi, I'm Joomy. This talk is about extracting programs from proofs --- but,
  unlike usual extraction, \emph{keeping the types}.\\[1ex]
  One-line pitch: dependently typed languages let you \emph{prove} things about
  code; but to \emph{run} that code you extract it, and today extraction throws
  all the rich types away. I'll show a \emph{verified} translation from the
  Calculus of Constructions into a \emph{typed} target --- System~F$\omega$ plus
  a dynamic type and blame --- that keeps as much type structure as it can, and
  marks the rest explicitly. All of it is mechanized in Rocq, with no admits.
}

\begin{frame}{Extraction throws the types away}
  Dependently typed languages (Rocq, Agda, Lean, Idris) let us \emph{prove}
  properties of code. To \emph{run} it, extraction erases the static parts:
  \[
    \text{Gallina} \;\longrightarrow\; \text{OCaml / Haskell / Scheme}
  \]

  \pause
  \medskip
  This makes \alert{two concessions}:
  \begin{enumerate}
    \item<3-> The target types \alert<3>{cannot express the source's dependent
      invariants} --- a verified program becomes an untyped-ish ML term whose
      safety is no longer in its type.
    \item<4-> The extractor joins the \alert<4>{trusted computing base} --- its
      correctness is argued informally.
  \end{enumerate}

  \onslide<5->{%
  \medskip
  Forster, Sozeau \& Tabareau (PLDI~'24) tackle \#2: verified extraction to
  OCaml. \quad\textbf{We tackle \#1.}}
  \note{%
    \textbf{[1:00 — 2:30]}\\
    Here's the setting. In Rocq, Agda, Lean, Idris we prove properties of code.
    But to run it we extract to OCaml, Haskell, Scheme --- and extraction erases
    the static parts.\\[1ex]
    \emph{(click)} That costs us two things.\\
    \emph{(click)} First: the target's types can't say what the source's types
    said. Your ``\texttt{Vec} of length \texttt{n}'' becomes just a list --- the
    proof that made it safe is gone from the type.\\
    \emph{(click)} Second: the extractor itself is trusted --- usually justified
    only on paper.\\
    \emph{(click)} There's great recent work --- Forster, Sozeau, Tabareau ---
    on the \emph{second} problem: a verified extractor, but to an untyped target.
    This talk is about the \emph{first} problem: can we extract into a
    \emph{typed} target and keep the structure? Pause here.
  }
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{The question, and this work}
  \begin{center}
    \large
    How much of a dependently typed program's structure can we
    \alert{preserve} when we extract into a \alert{typed} target ---\\[0.5ex]
    and can the translation be \alert{proved correct}?
  \end{center}

  \pause
  \medskip
  \begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Source:} \src{the Calculus of Constructions}, a pure type
      system --- mechanized on Barras' \emph{Coq-in-Coq}.
    \item<3-> \textbf{Target:} \tgt{System~F$\omega$} $+$ a dynamic type $\dyn$
      $+$ casts $+$ \textcolor{blameRed}{blame} --- a \emph{polymorphic blame
      calculus} (Ahmed--Findler--Siek--Wadler), lifted to higher kinds.
    \item<4-> \textbf{Lineage:} Paulin-Mohring (POPL~'89) extracted F$\omega$
      programs from CoC proofs; we return to that point with an explicit
      \emph{dynamic/blame} boundary.
  \end{itemize}
  \note{%
    \textbf{[2:30 — 4:00]}\\
    So the question, stated plainly: how much structure survives if the target
    is \emph{typed}, and can we \emph{prove} the translation right?\\[1ex]
    \emph{(click)} Source: the Calculus of Constructions --- full dependent
    types, as a pure type system, built on Barras' classic Coq-in-Coq.\\
    \emph{(click)} Target: System~F$\omega$ --- higher-kinded polymorphism ---
    plus a dynamic type ``Dyn'', casts, and blame. That's a \emph{polymorphic
    blame calculus}, in the Ahmed--Findler--Siek--Wadler line, but I lift it to
    higher kinds.\\
    \emph{(click)} And the lineage: Paulin-Mohring did CoC-to-F$\omega$ back in
    '89. We revisit that design point, but add an explicit dynamic/blame
    boundary for the parts F$\omega$ can't reach.
  }
\end{frame}

%% ==========================================================================
\section{The idea, by example}
%% ==========================================================================

\begin{frame}{The idea: be \emph{optimistic}}
  \tgt{F$\omega$} already preserves a lot: type abstraction, type application,
  higher-kinded families. \quad \src{CoC} has strictly more: \alert{full
  dependency} --- types may mention terms.

  \bigskip
  At the boundary, exactly three things happen:
  \begin{itemize}
    \item<2-> \tgt{\textbf{Preserve}} type structure --- type arguments stay as
      type applications; parametric skeleton survives.
    \item<3-> \textbf{Erase} term indices from types ---
      \src{\texttt{Vec A n}} $\rightsquigarrow$ \tgt{\texttt{Vec A}}.
    \item<4-> \textcolor{dynOrange}{\textbf{Dynamize}} values used \emph{as
      types} --- \src{\texttt{El : U -> Set}} $\rightsquigarrow$ $\dyn$ $+$
      \textcolor{blameRed}{blame}.
  \end{itemize}

  \onslide<5->{%
  \bigskip
  \begin{block}{Optimistic}
    Never defensively dynamize what F$\omega$ \emph{can} express. Reach for
    $\dyn$ only for the genuine residue of dependency.
  \end{block}}
  \note{%
    \textbf{[4:00 — 6:00] --- the conceptual core; slow down.}\\
    This is the heart of the talk. F$\omega$ already gives us a lot: polymorphism,
    type-level functions, higher-kinded families. CoC gives \emph{more} --- types
    can mention \emph{terms}. So a translation has to decide what to do with that
    extra dependency.\\[1ex]
    Three cases --- I'll show one example of each next.\\
    \emph{(click)} Preserve: anything that's really type structure stays type
    structure.\\
    \emph{(click)} Erase: an index like the \emph{length} of a vector is dropped
    \emph{from the type} --- \texttt{Vec A n} becomes \texttt{Vec A}. The value
    \texttt{n} may still be around as data; it's the \emph{invariant} that leaves.\\
    \emph{(click)} Dynamize: the hard residue --- a \emph{value used as a type},
    like a decoder \texttt{El : U -> Set} --- goes through Dyn with blame.\\
    \emph{(click)} The word ``optimistic'' is the design principle: we never
    dynamize defensively. We use Dyn \emph{only} where F$\omega$ genuinely can't
    express the thing.
  }
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}[fragile,shrink]{Erase: term indices leave the types}
  A bounds-checked vector lookup --- an ordinary \texttt{Definition} over the
  eliminators:
\begin{lstlisting}
Definition vnth (A : Set) (n : Nat) (xs : Vec A n) (i : Fin n)
  : A :=
  vrec A (fun (m : Nat) => Fin m -> A)
    (fun (i0 : Fin NZ) => fin0 A i0)
    (fun (m : Nat) (a : A) (rest : Fin m -> A) (j : Fin (NS m)) =>
       fin_case m A a rest j)
    n xs i.
\end{lstlisting}
  \pause
\begin{lstlisting}[style=replout]
fun (A : *) (n : Nat) (xs : Vec A) (i : Fin) =>
  vrec [A] [Fin -> A]
    (fun (x0 : Fin) => fin0 [A] x0)
    (fun (x0 : Nat) (x1 : A) (x2 : Fin -> A) (x3 : Fin) =>
       fin_case x0 [A] x1 x2 x3)
    n xs i
\end{lstlisting}
  \pause
  \src{\texttt{Vec A n}} $\rightsquigarrow$ \tgt{\texttt{Vec A}}, \
  \src{\texttt{Fin n}} $\rightsquigarrow$ \tgt{\texttt{Fin}}: the \alert{invariant}
  is gone, the implementation survives; \texttt{n}, \texttt{i} remain as data.
  \note{%
    \textbf{[6:00 — 7:30]}\\
    Concrete example of \emph{erase}. \texttt{vnth} looks up the \texttt{i}-th
    element of a length-\texttt{n} vector, with \texttt{i} provably in range ---
    a \texttt{Fin n}. It's just a definition built from the recursor; nothing
    exotic.\\[1ex]
    \emph{(click)} Here's the extraction. Look at the types in the shaded box:\\
    \emph{(click)} \texttt{Vec A n} became \texttt{Vec A}; \texttt{Fin n} became
    \texttt{Fin}. The dependent index \texttt{n} dropped out of the \emph{types}.
    But the \emph{code} is intact --- same recursion --- and \texttt{n} and
    \texttt{i} are still there as ordinary arguments. What we lost is exactly the
    \emph{invariant} ``\texttt{i} is in bounds,'' not the data. That's erasure.\\
    Type applications are the \texttt{[A]} in brackets --- note the polymorphism
    is preserved.
  }
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}[fragile]{Dynamize: where the dynamic type earns its keep}
  A Tarski universe: codes \src{\texttt{U}}, decoder \src{\texttt{El : U -> Set}}
  --- a function from \emph{data} to \emph{types}.
\begin{lstlisting}
Extract fun (c : U) => El c.
\end{lstlisting}
  \pause
\begin{lstlisting}[style=replout]
fun (c : U) =>
  cast<Dyn => U -> Dyn>^2+ (blame(2+)) c
\end{lstlisting}
  \pause
  \begin{itemize}
    \item \src{\texttt{El}} has \alert{no F$\omega$ image} --- it maps values to
      types.
    \item Extraction emits reserved \textcolor{blameRed}{internal blame} (label
      $2$), cast through $\dyn$ --- an \emph{honest} ``I can't translate this.''
    \item<4-> A theorem: an extracted term run in isolation \alert{never} reaches
      an \emph{external} label. (More on labels shortly.)
  \end{itemize}
  \note{%
    \textbf{[7:30 — 9:00]}\\
    Now \emph{dynamize} --- the interesting case. A Tarski universe: \texttt{U}
    is a type of \emph{codes}, and \texttt{El} decodes a code into an actual
    type. So \texttt{El} sends \emph{data} to \emph{types}. F$\omega$ has nothing
    like that.\\[1ex]
    \emph{(click)} The extraction of \texttt{fun c => El c} is this.\\
    \emph{(click)} Since \texttt{El} has no F$\omega$ counterpart, we don't fudge
    it --- we plant a \emph{blame}, cast through Dyn. It's a marked, honest
    failure: ``this used a value as a type; I can't express it.'' The label
    \texttt{2+} names \emph{which} kind of failure --- I'll unpack labels in two
    slides.\\
    \emph{(click)} And crucially, we \emph{prove} that a term we extract, run on
    its own, only ever trips these \emph{internal} markers --- never an external,
    user-level blame. So the fallback is contained.
  }
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}[fragile,shrink]{The example you'd expect to need
    \texorpdfstring{$\dyn$}{Dyn} --- and doesn't}
  CIC's poster child for a \emph{computed} return type:
\begin{lstlisting}
Fixpoint eval {t} (e : expr t)
  : match t with TNat => nat | TBool => bool end := ...
\end{lstlisting}
  \pause
  Church-encode the codes \alert{one kind up} ($\kstar\karr\kstar\karr\kstar$):
  \texttt{match} becomes type-level \emph{application} $t\,\mathrm{CNat}\,\mathrm{CBool}$.
\begin{lstlisting}[style=replout]
fun (t : (* => (* => *)))
    (x : t (forall (x0 : *), (x0 -> x0) -> x0 -> x0)
           (forall (x0 : *), x0 -> x0 -> x0)) =>
  x
\end{lstlisting}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item<3-> \texttt{tagless.v}: the interpreter extracts with \alert{no $\dyn$,
      no casts, no blame} --- the higher-kinded target expresses it natively.
    \item<4-> Forcing $\dyn$ as a computed return type needs \alert{large
      elimination} in the source --- the CIC extension.
  \end{itemize}
  \note{%
    \textbf{[9:00 — 10:30] --- optional; skip if behind schedule.}\\
    A ``gotcha'' slide. The program everyone expects to \emph{force} a dynamic
    type is the tagless interpreter: its \emph{return type} is computed by a
    \texttt{match} on a run-time tag --- \texttt{nat} or \texttt{bool}.\\[1ex]
    \emph{(click)} But if you Church-encode the codes one kind \emph{up}, the
    \texttt{match} becomes ordinary type-level \emph{application}, and F$\omega$
    eats it for free.\\
    \emph{(click)} The whole interpreter extracts with \emph{no} Dyn, \emph{no}
    casts, \emph{no} blame --- it lands in the static fragment.\\
    \emph{(click)} You only truly force Dyn as a computed \emph{return} type when
    the source has \emph{large elimination} --- which bare CoC lacks. So the
    boundary is sharper than folklore suggests. If I'm short on time I'll just
    say this sentence and move on.
  }
\end{frame}

\section{The target: F\texorpdfstring{$\omega$}{omega} + blame}
%% ==========================================================================

\begin{frame}{Target syntax}
  \begin{align*}
    K &::= \kstar \mid K \karr K &&\text{(kinds)}\\
    \onslide<2->
    A, B &::= \alpha \mid A \to B \mid \forall \alpha{:}K.\,A
      \mid \Lambda \alpha{:}K.\,A \mid A\ B \mid \dyn &&\text{(types)}\\
    \onslide<3->
    e &::= x \mid \lambda x{:}A.\,e \mid e\ e
      \mid \Lambda \alpha{:}K.\,e \mid e\,[A]\\
    &\quad \mid \castt{e}{A}{B}{p} \mid \gnd{e}{G} \mid \isgnd{e}{G}
      \mid \blame{p} \mid \nub{\alpha}{K}{A}{e} &&\text{(terms)}
  \end{align*}

  \begin{itemize}
    \item<4-> $\dyn$ is a proper type of kind $\kstar$ (no kind-indexed family).
    \item<5-> Ground types $G ::= \dyn \to \dyn \mid N$, \ $N ::= \alpha \mid N\,A$
      \alert{neutral} --- the F$\omega$ generalization of Blame-for-All's
      variable tag.
  \end{itemize}
  \note{%
    \textbf{[10:30 — 12:00]}\\
    The target in one slide. \emph{(click through the grammar.)}\\
    Kinds are the usual F$\omega$ kinds. Types are F$\omega$ --- variables,
    arrows, $\forall$, type-level $\Lambda$, application --- plus \emph{Dyn}.
    Terms are F$\omega$ terms plus four gradual-typing gadgets: a \emph{cast}
    from A to B with a label; a \emph{ground injection} and \emph{ground test}
    that mediate Dyn; a \emph{blame}; and a $\nu$ binder that seals a type ---
    I'll come back to that.\\[1ex]
    \emph{(click)} Two design points worth flagging: Dyn lives only at kind
    star --- there's no \(\mathrm{Dyn}_K\) for every kind.\\
    \emph{(click)} And ``ground types'' --- the shapes Dyn can hold --- now
    include \emph{neutral} types, a variable applied to arguments. That's the
    lift from System~F to F$\omega$: once type \emph{applications} can sit at the
    Dyn boundary, the tags have to cover them. Don't dwell; it motivates the
    next two slides.
  }
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}[shrink]{Reading a blame label}
  A cast that can't reconcile its two types does \alert{not} get stuck and does
  \alert{not} return a wrong value --- it fires $\blame{p}$. The label $p$ says:
  \begin{center}
  \begin{tabular}{ll}
    \textbf{id} & \emph{which} cast boundary broke \\
    \textbf{polarity} & \emph{whose} fault: \ $+$ the value in the cast, \
      $-$ the context around it \\
  \end{tabular}
  \end{center}

  \onslide<2->{%
  \smallskip
  Most ids are real boundaries; three are \alert{reserved} for the translation's
  own plumbing:
  \begin{center}
  \begin{tabular}{cl}
    \toprule
    id & fires when \ldots \\
    \midrule
    \textcolor{blameRed}{0} & a sealed type ($\nu$) would escape its scope through $\dyn$ \\
    \textcolor{blameRed}{1} & the ground test $\mathsf{is\_gnd}$ peeks at a sealed value \\
    \textcolor{blameRed}{2} & the extraction hits a construct with \alert{no F$\omega$ image} \ (${=}\,\lint$) \\
    \tgt{$\ge 3$} & \tgt{external} --- a genuine boundary a client set up \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
  \end{center}}

  \onslide<3->{%
  \begin{alertblock}{The safety theorem, in one line}
    An extracted term run in isolation only blames \textcolor{blameRed}{0},
    \textcolor{blameRed}{1}, or \textcolor{blameRed}{2} --- its own plumbing ---
    \alert{never} an external label ($\ge 3$).
  \end{alertblock}}
  \note{%
    \textbf{[12:00 — 14:00] --- audience asks about this; take your time.}\\
    Blame labels demystified. When a cast can't make its two types agree, it
    doesn't get stuck and doesn't lie --- it fires \texttt{blame p}. The label is
    a \emph{name tag} on the failure, and it answers two questions.\\
    The \emph{id} says which cast boundary broke. The \emph{polarity}, a plus or
    minus, says whose fault: plus blames the value flowing through the cast,
    minus blames the surrounding context. (That's the Wadler--Findler
    convention; a function cast flips the sign on its argument.)\\[1ex]
    \emph{(click)} Now the numbers. Most ids name real, programmer-level
    boundaries. But three small ids are reserved for our own machinery:\\
    0 --- a sealed type variable trying to escape its scope through Dyn;\\
    1 --- the ground test peeking at a sealed value;\\
    2 --- the big one from the El slide: the extraction hit something with no
    F$\omega$ image.\\
    Anything 3 or above is \emph{external} --- a boundary a client set up.\\
    \emph{(click)} And that split is the whole point: we prove an extracted term,
    run in isolation, can only ever blame 0, 1, or 2 --- its own plumbing ---
    never an external label. The extraction never frames an outside party.
  }
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}[shrink]{Executable compatibility: no stuck casts}
  Unlike Blame-for-All's \emph{consistency}, our $\mathsf{compat}(A,B)$ is
  \alert{executable} --- every constructor \emph{is} a cast-reduction rule:
  \begin{center}\small
  \begin{tabular}{ll}
    \toprule
    constructor & reduction rule \\
    \midrule
    \tgt{$\mathsf{compat\_refl}$} & \textcolor{accentOrange}{ID} \\
    \tgt{$\mathsf{compat\_arrow}$} & \textcolor{accentOrange}{WRAP} (contra/covariant) \\
    \tgt{$\mathsf{compat\_all}$} / \tgt{generalize} / \tgt{instantiate} & \textcolor{accentOrange}{$\forall$-steps} (instantiate at $\kstar$) \\
    \tgt{$\mathsf{compat\_to\_dyn}$} / \tgt{from\_dyn} & \textcolor{accentOrange}{GROUND / COLLAPSE / CONFLICT} \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
  \end{center}

  \begin{itemize}
    \item<2-> Cast to $\dyn$ injects at a ground tag; cast from $\dyn$ collapses
      on a match, \textcolor{blameRed}{blames} on a mismatch.
    \item<3-> So every well-typed cast \emph{steps}: \alert{no ``stuck cast''}.
      \mech{progress} falls out of \mech{compat\_dec}; it also makes the
      extraction's $\mathsf{coerce}$ \emph{total}.
    \item<4-> Caveat: $\mathsf{compat}$ is syntactic, not closed under
      definitional equality.
  \end{itemize}
  \note{%
    \textbf{[14:00 — 15:30]}\\
    How casts actually run. In System~F, Blame-for-All's ``consistency'' relation
    can be read both as a spec and as a strategy. Once you add type-level
    functions, that stops being obvious --- so we make it \emph{executable}:
    every constructor of our \texttt{compat} relation corresponds to a concrete
    reduction rule. Identity casts, function WRAP, the $\forall$ steps, and the
    Dyn injection/projection rules.\\
    \emph{(click)} The Dyn mechanics: casting \emph{to} Dyn injects the value at
    a ground tag; casting \emph{from} Dyn checks the tag --- collapses if it
    matches, \emph{blames} if it doesn't. That blame-on-mismatch is where runtime
    failures come from.\\
    \emph{(click)} The payoff: there's no catch-all ``stuck cast'' case. Every
    well-typed cast has a step, so \emph{progress} is basically the decision
    procedure for \texttt{compat}; and the same decidability makes the
    extraction's coercion a \emph{total} function.\\
    \emph{(click)} Honest caveat: \texttt{compat} is syntactic --- it doesn't see
    through definitional equality. That matters in a minute.
  }
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Target metatheory --- all mechanized, admit-free}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item<1-> \textbf{Determinism} (\mech{step\_deterministic}) --- proving it

\section{The extraction \& its metatheory}
%% ==========================================================================

\begin{frame}{The extraction: three maps, one invariant}
  \begin{description}
    \item<1->[Kinds] classifier domains become kind arrows, term domains drop:\\
      \src{$\SetS \to \Nat \to \SetS$} $\;\rightsquigarrow\;$
      \tgt{$\kstar \karr \kstar$} --- exactly what a \texttt{Vec}-like family needs.
    \item<2->[Types] $\extt(\Gamma, T) = \extt_L(\Gamma, \nf(T))$: \alert{normalize
      first}, then translate. Sorts / term variables / term-headed applications
      $\rightsquigarrow \dyn$.
    \item<3->[Terms] $\ext$ recurses on the \alert{typing derivation}.
  \end{description}

  \onslide<4->{%
  \medskip
  \begin{block}{The invariant that makes it well defined}
    Every ``type-level vs.\ term-level'' choice uses the reduction-stable,
    \emph{semantic} test $\islarge$ ($\hastype{\Gamma}{T}{\KindS}$) --- so the
    translation \alert{respects definitional equality}.
  \end{block}}
  \note{%
    \textbf{[16:30 — 18:00]}\\
    The translation is three maps working together. On kinds: a source binder
    whose domain is type-level becomes a kind arrow; a term-level domain is
    dropped --- that's how \texttt{Set -> Nat -> Set} becomes just star-to-star,
    the kind of a vector family.\\
    On types: the key move is \emph{normalize first}, then translate; sorts,
    term variables, and term-headed applications are what turn into Dyn.\\
    On terms: the extraction recurses on the \emph{typing derivation}, not the
    raw term --- it needs the typing information to classify binders. (Aside if
    asked: this forced \texttt{has\_type} to live in \texttt{Type}, not
    \texttt{Prop} --- and dragged the whole reduction/conversion layer into
    \texttt{Type} with it, since Rocq forbids eliminating \texttt{Prop} into
    \texttt{Type}.)\\[1ex]
    \emph{(click)} And the invariant that makes the whole thing well defined:
    every time we ask ``is this type-level or term-level?'' we use a
    \emph{semantic}, reduction-stable test --- ``does it have sort Kind?'' ---
    which is preserved by reduction. That's why the translation respects
    definitional equality instead of being brittle syntax.
  }
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{The hard case: it commutes only \emph{up to} conversion}
  For a large application (a type application, $\Lambda$-elimination), extraction
  emits a \alert{bare} $e\,[A]$ --- \emph{no} runtime cast. Why is that sound?

  \medskip
  Its natural type and the expected type,
  \[
    \extt(V{:}\Gamma, U_r)[\extt(\Gamma,v)/0]
    \qquad\text{vs.}\qquad
    \extt(\Gamma,\, U_r[v]),
  \]
  \pause
  are \alert{not syntactically equal} --- they differ by a target $\beta$-redex
  and by $\nf$ annotation drift. A naive commutation lemma with $=$ is
  \emph{false}.

  \pause
  \medskip
  They \emph{are} equal up to F$\omega$ definitional equality
  (\mech{ty\_equiv}). The target's conversion rule absorbs the gap ---
  which is exactly \emph{why} the target needs \texttt{typing\_conv}.
  \note{%
    \textbf{[18:00 — 19:30] --- the technical highlight; land it clearly.}\\
    My favorite subtlety. When the extraction hits a \emph{type} application, it
    just emits a bare type application in the target --- no cast, no coercion.
    You'd want to justify that with a lemma: ``extraction commutes with
    substitution.''\\[1ex]
    \emph{(click)} But phrased with plain \emph{equality}, that lemma is
    \emph{false}. The two sides differ two ways: plugging a large argument
    creates a target $\beta$-redex on one side, and normalizing annotations
    drifts them on the other.\\
    \emph{(click)} The fix: they're equal \emph{up to F$\omega$ definitional
    equality}. So the extraction leans on the target's \emph{conversion} rule to
    absorb the mismatch --- and that's the real reason the target has to carry a
    conversion rule at all. This is the kind of thing that only shows up when you
    actually mechanize it.
  }
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{The headline theorems (Rocq; only \texttt{eq\_rect\_eq})}
  \begin{enumerate}
    \item<1-> \textbf{Well-typed extraction} (\mech{extract\_well\_typed}):
      $\hastype{\extc(\Gamma)}{\ext(\Gamma \vdash t{:}T)}{\extt(\Gamma, T)}$.
    \item<2-> \textbf{Simulation} (\mech{extract\_reduces}): $t \reduces v$
      $\Rightarrow$ $\ext(t) \sepsim^{*} \ext(v)$.
    \item<3-> \textbf{Derivation independence} (\mech{extract\_deriv\_indep}):
      the extraction doesn't depend on \emph{which} derivation.
    \item<4-> \textbf{External-label non-generation}
      (\mech{extraction\_blame\_free}): in isolation, never $\blame{p}$ with
      $\mathrm{id}(p) \ge 3$.
    \item<5-> \textbf{Optimism / $\dyn$-freedom}
      (\mech{extract\_typ\_dyn\_free\_iff}): an \emph{exact} syntactic
      characterization of which source types extract without $\dyn$.
  \end{enumerate}
  \note{%
    \textbf{[19:30 — 21:00]}\\
    The payoff theorems --- all in Rocq, and the audited set rests on a single
    standard axiom, \texttt{eq\_rect\_eq}.\\
    One: extraction is \emph{well-typed} --- a well-typed source term extracts to
    a well-typed target term at the extracted type.\\
    Two: \emph{simulation} --- source reduction is tracked by the extraction.\\
    Three: it doesn't depend on which typing derivation you picked.\\
    Four: the safety result from the labels slide --- no external blame in
    isolation.\\
    Five: ``optimism'' made precise --- an \emph{exact} characterization of which
    source types come out Dyn-free. Read two or three of these out loud, don't
    grind through all five.
  }
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Being honest about what this is not}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item<1-> \textbf{Simulation is \emph{syntactic}}, not contextual
      approximation: $\sepsim$ has rules (like $\blame{q}\sepsim t$) that are
      false as behavioral principles. A typed logical-relations bridge is open.
    \item<2-> \textbf{Internal blame is reachable by design} --- a value-as-type
      run at runtime blames $\lint$. That's the marked fallback, not a bug.
    \item<3-> \textbf{Not proof-erasing}, \textbf{$\dyn$ only at kind $\kstar$},
      \textbf{inductives axiomatized} (bare PTS). All are future extensions.
  \end{itemize}

  None of these weakens the theorems proved --- they scope the claims.}

\begin{frame}[shrink]{\texttt{Inductive} by Böhm--Berarducci --- and its ceiling}
  \texttt{Inductive T params : arity := \ldots} desugars to the \alert{indexed}
  impredicative encoding, as computing \texttt{Definition}s:
  \[
    \tgt{\texttt{Vec A n}} \;=\; \forall (X{:}\Nat{\to}\kstar).\;
      X\,\mathrm{NZ} \to (\forall m.\,A \to X\,m \to X\,(\mathrm{NS}\,m)) \to X\,n
  \]
  \begin{itemize}
    \item<2-> Motive is \alert{index-parameterized} ($X : I{\to}\kstar$), so
      \tgt{Vec}, \tgt{Fin}, Leibniz \tgt{Eq} ($\mathit{Eq\_rec}$ \emph{is}
      transport), \tgt{Fmt}, $\le$ all become terms that \textbf{compute}
      (Böhm--Berarducci '85; Pfenning--Paulin '90).
    \item<3-> \alert{Ceiling 1}: the recursor depends on \emph{indices}, not the
      \emph{value} --- dependent \emph{induction} is not derivable (Geuvers '01).
      No large elimination (\texttt{vreplicate}), no dependent projections.
    \item<4-> \alert{Ceiling 2}: one-step \emph{inversion} (\texttt{Fin} case,
      HList head) is not a fold --- kept axiomatic.
    \item<5-> Scott/Parigot give O(1) inversion but need \emph{recursive types}
      (not SN here); Mendler widens functors, same ceiling; \textcolor{okGreen}{self
      types} (Fu--Stump '14) recover induction --- beyond CoC.
  \end{itemize}
  \note{%
    \textbf{[23:00 — 24:30] --- the encoding story; land the tension.}\\
    A bonus the REPL gives us: an \texttt{Inductive} command that's pure sugar
    for the impredicative Böhm--Berarducci encoding --- and it handles
    \emph{indexed} families, because the motive is parameterized by the index.
    So Vec, Fin, Leibniz equality (whose recursor is literally transport), the
    format family, the $\le$ predicate --- all become plain lambda-terms that
    \emph{compute} under \texttt{Compute}. Böhm--Berarducci in '85;
    Pfenning--Paulin encoded these in CoC in '90.\\[1ex]
    \emph{(click)} Now the honest ceiling, and it's intrinsic, not a bug. The
    generated recursor's motive depends on the \emph{indices} but not the
    \emph{value} --- it's a fold, not dependent induction, and Geuvers proved in
    2001 that induction simply isn't derivable from these encodings. So large
    elimination and dependent projections are out.\\
    \emph{(click)} Second, one-step inversion isn't a fold either, so those stay
    axiomatic.\\
    \emph{(click)} And the alternatives: Scott and Parigot give you O(1)
    inversion, but their types are recursive, so they don't even typecheck in a
    normalizing system without mu-types; Mendler handles more functors but hits
    the same wall; and if you really want dependent elimination with computation,
    that's self types --- Fu and Stump --- which is a genuine extension beyond
    CoC. This is the typeability-versus-computability tension in one slide.
  }
\end{frame}
